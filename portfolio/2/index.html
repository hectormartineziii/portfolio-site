The 737MAX rolls out, a reflection on my career and Boeing.

Today's rollout of the 737-8 MAX culminates two-years of effort designing the single-aisle airplane of Boeing's future and a celebration of my career advancement within the Boeing company.




I had served a long and successful tenure in commercial aviation services providing subject matter expertise to airlines so they can optimize their maintenance operations while keeping their fleet safe. After which I joined the 737-8 MAX project in Boeing's 10-20 building in Renton, Washington. Our team was small, and due to the large amount of work in front of us, growing.


We were so short staffed that I took on some work generally reserved for the more Senior Level 3 engineers. I was a Level 2 at the time. A few months’ time saw a move to the 4-21 building and the program in full swing, it was around this time that I began work on detail design. It was this role, and my performance in it, that led me to become the Lead Loads Engineer for the 737MAX flaps systems. with a nice Level 3, Senior Engineer, promotion to boot.


I see my role here as someone who balances the interests of both our customers and the company. Our customers desire a more fuel efficient airplane. This in turn allows airlines to compete on price because it's in a field that's not differentiated. The passengers in turn decide both on price and schedule. The company wants to meet customer needs at the lowest possible cost, fixed and variable. I have a direct impact on both fixed and variable costs of this development.


Do we need titanium bolts to hold the flaps together? Yes we do. But are there adjustments we can make to standard sizes, in thickness and length, that will allow the company to benefit from economies of scale across all their product lines? Yes! It’s questions like these that I consistently think about and unlocking these efficiencies gives me a sense of pride when I see this airplane roll out of the factory.


Good thing is, I get to do this all over again for the next derivative, the 737-9 MAX.
